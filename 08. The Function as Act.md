# Chapter VIII — The Function as Act
### *On Functions, the Hermetic Principle, and the Ethics of Limits*

---

The universe does not create a new sunrise every morning.

Think about that for a moment. The sunrise you see today is not a unique event specially manufactured for this particular Tuesday. The laws of physics — the rotation of the Earth at a specific angular velocity, the geometry of the Sun's photons entering the atmosphere at a particular angle, the scattering of blue light that makes the sky orange and pink — are the same laws that produced yesterday's sunrise, and the one before that, and all the sunrises since Earth had an atmosphere thick enough to scatter light.

The universe executes the same function, `sunrise()`, once per rotation. The function was defined once, at the dawn of physics. It has been called billions of times, once per day, once per planet, wherever the conditions match. And each call produces a result that is recognizably a sunrise while being completely particular — this light, this cloud configuration, this moment.

This is the deepest argument for why functions exist in programming: because the universe itself is organized around reusable patterns, and we are children of the universe.

---

## The Hermetic Principle of Correspondence

The Third Hermetic Principle, as described in the Kybalion, states: *"As above, so below; as below, so above; as within, so without; as without, so within."*

This principle describes correspondences — the idea that the same patterns appear at every level of reality, from the cosmic to the atomic, from the metaphysical to the physical. The atom is a solar system in miniature. The brain's neural network resembles the cosmic web of galactic filaments. Biological branching follows the same mathematical rules as river deltas and lightning bolts.

This is not coincidence. It is the universe expressing the same underlying principles at every scale.

The function is the programmer's tool for encoding these correspondences. When you write a function, you are saying: this pattern appears more than once. This pattern has a name. This pattern can be invoked. You are lifting the pattern out of the specific and giving it general form — which is exactly what the Hermetic tradition means by the correspondence between levels. The specific instance corresponds to the general principle, and the general principle finds expression in the specific instance.

`calculate_batting_average()` is a Hermetic function. It encodes a principle — the ratio of hits to at-bats — that is true for every batter who ever played. Invoke it with Yamil's numbers and it tells you his average. Invoke it with Ruth's numbers and it tells you Ruth's. Same principle, different invocation, different result. As above (the principle), so below (the instance).

## Yin and Yang of Definition and Invocation

A function has two moments, and they are philosophically distinct.

The first is *definition*. The moment when you write `def calculate_batting_average(hits, at_bats):` and describe what the function will do when called. In this moment, the function does not act. It exists but it does not manifest. It is potential without actuality, like the seed before it encounters soil and water. The function sits in the code, complete in its definition, waiting.

This is Yin: receptive, still, containing, potential. The function exists in quietude. It is the capability that has not yet been exercised, the knowledge that has not yet been applied, the strength that has not yet been called upon.

The second moment is *invocation*. The moment when elsewhere in the code you write `calculate_batting_average(47, 134)`, and the function's body executes, and an answer emerges, and the result returns to the caller and affects the program's state. In this moment, the potential becomes actual. The seed becomes the plant. The capability becomes the action.

This is Yang: active, expressive, directed, manifesting. The function moves through its logic. It computes. It returns. It changes things.

The Tao Te Ching captures this exactly: *"The Tao does not act, and yet nothing is left undone."* The function, in its definition, does not act. Yet everything the function will ever do is already contained in it. When it is invoked, nothing needs to be invented — only released. The action flows from the definition the way water flows from the source. The definition is the Tao; the invocation is the ten thousand things the Tao becomes.

## The Docstring as Conscious Memory

A docstring is the text that appears immediately after a function's definition, enclosed in triple quotes, describing what the function does, what it receives, and what it returns. Technically, it is documentation. Philosophically, it is something more.

The docstring is the function's memory of its own purpose.

There is a kind of amnesia that afflicts code written without documentation. You return to it six months later and find yourself staring at a function called `process()` that takes an argument called `data` and returns something called `result`. You have no idea what it does. You have to reverse-engineer it — to become an archaeologist of your own past thinking. This is an act of failure, not just of convenience but of responsibility. You failed the future person who would read the code. And the future person, in this case, is you.

The docstring is the act of responsible memory. It says: *this function exists to do X. Here is what it expects. Here is what it returns. Here is why it exists rather than some other approach.* The docstring does not describe *how* the function works — the code does that. It describes *why* the function exists, which is the more important question, and the one the code cannot answer.

The Stoics distinguished between *episteme* (scientific knowledge, knowing how) and *phronesis* (practical wisdom, knowing why and when). Good code has episteme — it works, and a careful reader can understand how. Good documentation adds phronesis — it explains the judgment behind the design, the problem that motivated the solution, the context in which the function should and should not be used.

Write docstrings. Not because they are required. Because they are the responsible exercise of memory in a collaborative art.

## The Ethics of Scope

Every function has a scope — a region of the code within which its local variables are visible and outside which they are not. A variable defined inside a function cannot be accessed from outside that function (unless explicitly returned or referenced). This is not arbitrary restriction. It is architectural wisdom.

The philosophical principle behind scope is *appropriate limits*. Not all information should be available everywhere. Not all power should be exercised at all times. The concept of *information hiding* — encapsulation, in the language of object-oriented design — is the recognition that a well-designed system gives each component access only to what it needs and no more.

This is the Hermetic principle of initiatory knowledge applied to programming: different levels of access correspond to different levels of need and responsibility. The intern at a hospital does not have access to all patient records, not because the hospital is being secretive, but because access without need is access that creates risk without benefit. The scope of the function is the hospital's access control for variables.

But the ethics go deeper. The function that reaches outside its scope — that modifies global state without being asked, that creates side effects that are not apparent from its name and signature — is an untrustworthy function. It is like a person who, asked to complete a specific task, takes it upon themselves to also rearrange your furniture and answer your emails. They meant well. The result is still chaos.

A well-designed function does exactly what its name says. No more. No less. And it communicates everything that comes in and goes out through its defined parameters and return values — nothing hidden, nothing unexpected. This predictability is not rigidity. It is integrity. You can trust a function that tells you everything it does. You cannot trust one that does things it does not mention.

## Ashe: The Yoruba Power of Manifestation

In the Yoruba spiritual tradition, *Ashe* (Àṣẹ) is the divine power that makes things happen — the force that transforms intention into reality, potential into act. It is the vital energy that moves through the cosmos, through living beings, through ritual and prayer, causing things to come into being. When a priest invokes a ceremony, they are using Ashe. When a mother blesses her child, she is channeling Ashe. When a community comes together with shared intention, their combined Ashe makes possible what individual effort could not.

When you invoke a function, you are performing a computational act of Ashe.

The function exists in potential — its definition is the intention, the capacity, the knowledge of what is to be done. But it does nothing until invoked. The invocation is the Ashe — the moment when potential becomes actual, when the described action becomes the performed action, when the blueprint becomes the building.

And notice: the Ashe flows from the *caller* to the *function*. The function does not choose to act. It is *called* into action by the surrounding code. This is a distributed act — the function depends on being needed, and the caller depends on the function's capability. Neither alone can produce the result. Together they complete the cycle.

This is why the programming community uses the word *calling* a function — not *using* it, not *running* it, not *executing* it. Calling. As if summoning. As if invoking a presence that was waiting to be needed.

## Single Responsibility: The Monk's Vow

The most important principle governing function design is what Robert C. Martin called the Single Responsibility Principle: a function should do one thing, and do it well. If you can honestly describe what a function does using the word "and" — "it validates the input *and* calculates the result *and* saves to the database" — then it is not one function. It is three.

The monk takes vows. Each vow is specific. Chastity. Poverty. Obedience. Not "be good in general" — specific commitments that constrain specific aspects of life. The specificity is the point. A vow that covers everything covers nothing. A function that does everything does nothing reliably.

The function that does one thing does it clearly, does it testably, does it in a way that can be understood by a fresh reader in thirty seconds. It can be given a name that accurately describes it — and this accuracy is the test. If you cannot name a function without "and," refactor it until you can.

The Zen monk's calligraphy: one brushstroke, made with complete presence, containing everything it needs to contain and nothing it does not. The Single Responsibility Principle is the calligraphy of code.
